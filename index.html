Oh SNAP! On Food Stamps

The Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) is a social program enacted by American government in the 1960s to combat hunger in the United States; it is more commonly known as food stamps, referring to the way that the aid was disbursed with special coupons before the switch to an Electronic Benefit Transfer (EBT) system. In this visualization, we'll explore a brief history of the program as well as examine how the program benefits states individually.

SNAP Variations by State

Though SNAP was initiated at the Federal Level, the States each choose to implement their own versions of SNAP, with different application requirements and income cutoffs. Along with inherent demographic variations, this has led to a large amount of variation among the 50 states in terms of who is benefitting from the program and how much. For example, the average benefits in Hawaii and Alaksa are much higher than those in much of rest of the United States.

How does SNAP affect each state?

Under federal law, there are three criteria that every household must meet in order to qualify for benefits under SNAP.

  • Household gross monthly income must be at most 130% of the federal poverty line unless the household includes an elderly or disabled member.
  • Household net monthly income after deductions must be at or below the federal poverty line
  • Assets must be under limits that depend upon if the household includes an elderly or disabled member

These limitations, however, can be adjusted to some degree by the state implementing the food stamp program.

A few other areas in which states are allowed flexibility in their implementation of SNAP include:

  • The specific name of the program (CalFresh in California vs. Nutrition Assistance in Arizona)
  • When and how often benefit recipients must report their current household situations
  • Whether all of the household changes will be acted upon or not
  • Whether child support payments that are legally obligated are included as a deduction vs. an income exclusion

Donald Trump's Recent SNAP Rule Changes

Recently, the Trump administration created new work requirements for those who receive food snaps as beneficiaries of the Supplementary Nutrition Assistance Program. The new rules remove some of the states' flexibility of waiving certain working requirements for individuals to qualify for food stamps to match their states' needs. As a result of these new requirements, nearly 700,000 Americans will no longer have access to a basic need.
